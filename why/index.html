About this site

The Vault Solves the Wrong Problem

Most JIT access content is written by the vendors selling it: legacy PAM platforms defending the vault architecture their engineering teams are built around, and cloud-native challengers selling ephemeral tokens as a category they happen to own. The security leader trying to figure out whether they need a JIT overlay, a PAM replacement, or something else entirely has no neutral ground to stand on. This site is an attempt to fix that.

Written for the buyer caught between two incumbents

Legacy PAM vendors are not wrong that JIT can be delivered as a vault capability. Time-limited session brokering through an audited gateway eliminates standing access while preserving the architecture their customers already run. What that framing leaves out is that the vault itself is still present, still reachable, and its credentials still exist between sessions.

Cloud-native JIT challengers are not wrong that ephemeral tokens are architecturally cleaner. A credential that does not exist cannot be stolen. What that framing leaves out is the hybrid estate: legacy on-premises databases, industrial infrastructure, Active Directory workloads that cannot absorb cloud IAM semantics. Zero standing privilege in the cloud-native slice of the environment is a narrower win than the pitch implies when the rest of the estate still runs on vault-brokered access.

Both sides are selling the architecture they already have. Neither has an incentive to describe the full tradeoff, because doing so narrows the accounts they can close. The result is a market where most buyer-facing content is accurate within its frame and incomplete outside it.

Standing privilege is what makes the third number possible. A credential that exists continuously gives an attacker, or a compromised script, months of cover before anyone notices the access was never supposed to be there.

What zero standing privilege actually requires

ZSP is the right target. The question is not whether it's correct, but whether a given platform gets you there across your full estate or only the part it was built for. For cloud-native workloads, ephemeral token generation through an IAM-native platform is a practical path. For hybrid environments, the realistic outcome is usually a combination: ephemeral tokens where the infrastructure supports them, vault-brokered JIT with short windows where it doesn't. That combination is not a failure state. It is a more accurate description of where most enterprise environments actually sit.

The operational problems that don't make it into vendor decks, zombie sessions that outlive token expiration, policy sprawl across heterogeneous cloud accounts, approval latency that breaks on-call response, are the problems this site is built to address.

The machine identity complication

The fastest-growing JIT use case isn't a human engineer requesting prod access. It's a service account, a CI/CD pipeline, or an agent that needs a millisecond-latency credential to touch one resource. Human approval workflows don't scale to that velocity. This splits the market in two: platforms built for human access aren't the right architecture for workload JIT, and platforms built for workload JIT don't cover the human use case. Both call themselves JIT access platforms. A buyer evaluating both on the same shortlist may not realize they're solving different problems until well into a proof of concept.

JIT access and non-human identity governance are converging on the same underlying problem from two different category names. This site covers both, with explicit content on where they overlap and where they don't. The sibling site nonhumanidentitysoftware.com covers the NHI side in more depth.

What independent means on this site

This site does not favor vault-brokered architecture over ephemeral tokens, or cloud-native platforms over hybrid-capable ones. Every approach is evaluated against the environment it actually targets. Analysis is based on public documentation, practitioner input, and direct evaluation where possible. Where the evidence for a claim is thin, this site says so.

The people making JIT decisions are trying to close the gap between a security principle everyone agrees on and an environment too heterogeneous for any single vendor's architecture to fully cover. They need better information than the market currently gives them. That is the only reason this site exists.
